## Purchase Terms

You asked me to read these as a buyer, so here's my candid take card by card. Overall they read clearly and the layout (four cards: Deposit / Finance / Settlement / Build) is easy to scan. The framing line — "Indicative contract terms... full contract documentation provided at contract stage... all terms may be varied by mutual agreement" — is exactly right. It sets expectations without over-promising.

- **Deposit 5% — "Payable within 5 days of contract."** Clear and standard. A 5% deposit on exchange is normal; the 5-day window is fine. No confusion.
- **Finance 30 days — "Finance approval window from date of contract."** This reads correctly to me as a finance clause: I'd have 30 days from signing to get unconditional finance approval. Good. One thing an experienced buyer wonders: is the contract *subject to* finance (i.e. I can walk and get my deposit back if finance falls through), or is this just an expected timeframe? The phrase "finance approval window" is slightly ambiguous on that point. The protection (subject-to-finance) is the thing buyers actually care about.
- **Settlement "On Title" — "Settlement after the issue of title."** This is the most important one for an off-the-plan / new-subdivision buyer and you've labelled it well. "On Title" tells me settlement happens once the title for my lot issues — which in a new subdivision can be months out and is outside my control. That's honest and correct. I'd just add the implication: that the completion date is therefore *estimated* and tied to titling, so I shouldn't book removalists off a fixed date.
- **Build "Modular" — "Factory-built modules, ~12–14 weeks from site arrival."** Good that you're upfront that these are modular/factory-built — some buyers care a lot, and burying it would be a trust mistake. "~12–14 weeks from site arrival" is a nice concrete number. The thing it raises and doesn't answer: modular construction sometimes affects lending (some banks treat modular/transportable differently). If your finance partners are comfortable with it, saying so here would defuse a real worry.
- The closing line — "Settlement is on a turnkey basis — you take possession of a complete, built home" — is excellent and exactly what a buyer wants to hear. Turnkey vs progress-payment is a genuine fork in the road and you've come down clearly on the reassuring side. (Worth noting it sits slightly in tension with the modular "12–14 weeks from site arrival" detail — turnkey means I pay on completion, not progress draws, which is great; just make sure the contract genuinely matches the turnkey promise.)

### The 3-home limit (you specifically asked me to test the 4th pick)

I tested it properly. I selected U1, then U3, then U9 — all three registered as my first, second and third choice and appeared in the form. Then I clicked a **fourth** home (U11). Result: **U11 was NOT added** — the selection stayed at exactly U1, U3, U9 — and a notice appeared: *"You can register interest in up to 3 homes — your first, second and third choice. Deselect one to pick another."* That's the correct behaviour and a genuinely good message — it doesn't just block me, it tells me *how to proceed* (deselect one). Well done; this is the kind of thing that's easy to get wrong and you got it right.

Two honest caveats from the test:
- The notice is **transient** — it fades after a couple of seconds. If I'd glanced away when I clicked the 4th home, I'd have seen nothing happen and wondered if the map was broken ("I clicked U11 and nothing happened"). I'd let that message persist until I either deselect something or dismiss it, rather than auto-vanishing.
- The notice isn't wired to a screen-reader live region (no `aria-live`), so a vision-impaired buyer using a reader wouldn't be told why their fourth click did nothing. Same fix as above — a polite live region — solves both the "blink and miss it" and the accessibility gap at once.

Opportunity: Beyond the cap, the labelling of selections is good ("first/second/third choice") but I'd lean into *why* I'd pick three. A one-liner — "If your first choice is allocated to someone else, we'll offer your second, then third" — turns three arbitrary picks into a sensible insurance strategy I understand. Right now I'm guessing why three.

Opportunity: Two small upgrades. (1) Rename "No price set" to "Add your price expectation" and add a soft anchor ("similar packages typically $XXX–$XXX") so I'm not guessing into a void. A buyer's last hesitation before hitting submit is "what am I unleashing?" Answer it right above the button.

## Other Strategic Feature Suggestions

- **Price is the missing front door.** I'll say it once more because it's the one thing that would change my behaviour most: with no price or band anywhere, a serious buyer can't qualify themselves in or out. Even an indicative "from $XXX,000, subject to confirmation" — consistent with all your other honest caveats — converts browsers into registrants. Without it, you're collecting interest from people who haven't checked they can afford it, which wastes everyone's time later.
- **Per-home detail, not estate-wide ranges.** Land size, aspect/orientation, and (eventually) any price difference should attach to *each* unit in the hover card and the selected-homes list. I'm choosing a specific block, not "the estate."
- **A "what's included" / inclusions summary.** Turnkey is promised, but turnkey means different things to different builders. A short inclusions list (flooring, landscaping, driveway, fencing, appliances, blinds?) would pre-empt the single most common new-home dispute: "I thought that was included."
- **Plain-English differentiators between the five plans.** As covered — one line each so I can actually choose.
- **"What happens next" after registration.** Set the expectation: confirmation email, allocation process, no hard-sell. Reduces the last-second submit hesitation.
- **A voice/assistant or live chat for contract questions.** Right now my only channel for a nuanced question ("is the contract subject to finance?", "do banks lend on modular here?") is to email and wait. A small "ask a question" assistant on the page — even one that answers the common FAQs and hands the rest to you — would catch buyers at peak intent. I didn't find any assistant surface anywhere on the page.
