# Seafields Estate — walkthrough notes

**Tester:** Anneke (25+ yrs WA residential land sales / estate launches)
**URL:** https://f2k-projects.vercel.app/seafields-estate
**Viewport:** laptop, 1440px
**Date:** 25 May 2026

A note before I start: I walked this the way I'd walk a display-village screen with a buyer beside me and a selling agent's eye over my shoulder. I'm generous but I'll tell you plainly where I'm not a fan. The good news first — this is one of the more polished estate pages I've seen out of a developer. It's clean, the copy is sober and credible, the staged-release story is told well, and the registration journey is genuinely thought-through. Most of what I found is detail and data, not structure. But a couple of the data issues would embarrass you in front of a buyer, so they matter.

---

## Hero + header

What's there: a dark-green header with the F2K logo, nav (Projects / Seafields / Branscombe / Hemp Homes / About F2K), a sticky "REGISTRATION OF INTEREST ONLY — no deposit required or accepted" banner across the very top, then "Seafields Estate", the 145-lot / land-or-H&L subhead, "Waggrakine, Geraldton WA — 8km from Geraldton CBD", a paragraph, a green "Select your lot →" CTA, and a colourful subdivision-plan thumbnail. A stats strip sits under it: 145 Lots · 445–1522m² · From $155k land · From $485k H&L · From Q3 2026.

This is a strong first impression. The disclaimer banner up top is the right instinct — it sets expectations before a buyer even reads the headline, which heads off the "have I just committed to something?" anxiety. The tab title reads "Seafields Estate — Register Your Interest | F2K", so that's done properly.

One thing my eye went straight to, because I count lots for a living: **the page says 145 lots in three places (hero, subhead, stats, total line), but the lot data behind the map only contains 143.** More on that under the map — but flag it now, because a headline number that doesn't reconcile is the first thing a sharp buyer or a competing agent will pick at.

**Opportunity:** the hero thumbnail is a flat coloured plan. A single aerial or a render of a finished modular home on a flat Geraldton block would do more emotional work here than the schematic — land buyers buy the lifestyle picture first and the polygon second.

## About the development

"Land & Lifestyle in Geraldton's Growth Corridor", a solid two-paragraph story (part of a ~300-lot development, 155+ sold since 2012, flat lots, services connected before titling ~Sept 2026, the $188M Geraldton Health Campus as a demand driver), and a clean spec table: Developer, Location (Pepper Gate, Waggrakine 6530), Zoning R20, Lots 145, Sizes 445–1522 (avg 610), Land Area 8.84ha, Terrain Flat, Services, Planner CLE, Plan Reference (CLE 3027-08B-01, WAPC 202888), Covenant.

This is exactly the information I'd want on a fact sheet, and the "155 lots already sold since 2012" line is good, credible social proof — it tells a buyer this isn't a punt on a greenfield first stage. The Health Campus hook is the right local lever. Good explanatory framing throughout.

One operator nit: "over 155 lots already sold" in a 300-lot development, then "145 residential lots" here — a first-time buyer can muddle "is this estate 145 or 300?". The copy does explain it ("part of a larger ~300-lot development"), but I'd tighten it so the 145 is unmistakably "what's left to sell here."

**Opportunity:** add a one-line distance-to-amenity list (schools, the new hospital campus, beach, Geraldton CBD drive time). "8km from CBD" is good; "6 min to the new $188M hospital, 4 min to X Primary" is what converts a healthcare-worker buyer.

## Development staging (Stages 1–7)

Header: "Stages 1–3 Open Now — 43 Lots", a clear paragraph explaining the seven-stage release, that Stage 2 is a single retained heritage lot not for sale, and the line I genuinely liked: *"Pricing is set by lot size, not by stage, so registering early gives you the best pick of available lots without paying a stage premium."* Then seven stage cards — Stage 1 OPEN (20, SW Block), Stage 2 RESERVED (1, Heritage), Stage 3 OPEN (23, Central), Stages 4–7 LOCKED — all "From $155k".

This is the best-told part of the page. The "no stage premium, register early for best pick" line is a textbook scarcity-without-pressure play, and it's true to how staged releases actually work. The locked/open/reserved visual language is instantly readable.

But the counts wobble, and I'll be specific because I checked them against your own lot feed:
- The staging card says **Stage 1 = 20 lots**; the lot data says **19**.
- The staging card says **Stage 3 = 23**; the map's own stage filter says **24**; the data says **24**. So your two on-page numbers for Stage 3 already disagree with each other (23 vs 24).
- The map filter says **Stage 6 = 34**; the data says **33**.
- Add the on-page stage numbers and you get 145; add the actual data and you get 143.

Buyers and agents *do* add these up. When the headline says "43 lots open across Stages 1 & 3" but the two cards say 20 + 23 = 43 while the map says 20 + 24 = 44, someone's going to ask "so how many are actually available?" — and right now you can't answer cleanly. This isn't cosmetic; it's the credibility of the scarcity story.

**Opportunity:** drive every count off the single lot feed so the hero number, the stage cards, the map filter pills, and the "X lots open" line can never disagree. One source of truth, computed, not typed.

## Two ways to buy / Geraldton market

"Vacant Serviced Land" (from $155,000) and "House & Land Package" (turnkey modular, from $485,000) as two cards, then a market strip: $533K median (Waggrakine), 27% annual growth, <1% rental vacancy.

Clean and persuasive. The <1% vacancy + 27% growth combo is a strong investor pitch and it's right next to the "Investor" buyer-type the form captures — good alignment. The $485k H&L "from" figure is the one to remember, because the Home Designs section and a lot's own modal don't honour it (see below).

**Opportunity:** cite a source/date on the market stats ("REIWA, Q1 2026") — investors discount un-sourced growth figures, and a date protects you when the numbers move.

## Factory2Key Home Designs — the section you asked me to look hardest at

Header "Modular Homes Built to Plan" with a good explanatory lead (factory-built, delivered as modules, pick a design at registration and get quoted against your lot, 12–14 weeks from site arrival). Six cards: Joey, Koala, 3x2 Modular, 4x2 Modular, BigRoo, Wombat — each with a floor-plan preview, badges, and a price line.

The cards look good and the floor plans open in a new tab (I checked — every "View plan" / "Elevations" / "Option 2" link returns a live file, no dead links). But there are real problems here a buyer will hit:

**1. The pricing labels aren't comparable, and that's confusing.** Look at them side by side:
- Joey — "House only — from $297,900"
- Koala — "House only — from $327,700"
- 3x2 Modular — "Price on application"
- 4x2 Modular — "H&L from $680,000"
- BigRoo — "H&L from $829,700"
- Wombat — "Pricing TBC"

Four different pricing conventions across six cards. Joey/Koala quote the *build only* (no land). 4x2/BigRoo quote *house + land*. Two are "on application / TBC". A buyer cannot line these up. "Joey $297,900" sitting next to "BigRoo $829,700 H&L" reads as if the gap is the house, when actually one excludes land and one includes it. I'd be doing mental arithmetic at the kitchen table to explain this to a client, and I shouldn't have to. Pick ONE convention — I'd show every card as "House only from $X" *and* "H&L from $Y (incl. a from-$155k lot)" so they're apples to apples.

**2. The $485k promise has no home to land on.** The hero and "Two Ways to Buy" both say "House & Land from $485,000". The cheapest actual H&L figure in the designs is the 4x2 at $680,000. Joey/Koala are "house only". So a buyer who clicks through chasing "$485k H&L" can't find it. Either there's a small/ADU H&L package around $485k that isn't shown, or the headline number is stale. Right now it's a promise the section doesn't keep.

**3. Koala's floor-plan thumbnail is actually Joey's plan.** The Koala card's preview image is literally `joey-floor-plan.png` — the same file Joey uses. So a buyer comparing the two ancillary designs sees identical plans on screen (the *link* opens the correct koala PDF, but the inline preview is wrong). For two products you're positioning as "Koala is slightly larger than Joey", showing the same picture undercuts the whole point.

**4. BigRoo's plan is filed as "murchison".** BigRoo's card says "Floor plan pending", yet its "View plan" link opens `murchison-floor-plan.png` — a different name entirely. Either BigRoo is internally "Murchison" (then say so, or rename the file) or the wrong plan is attached. And "Floor plan pending" while a plan link exists is a mixed message.

**5. The GROH badges are good — lean into them.** The 3x2 and 4x2 carry "GROH ELIGIBLE" badges, and the "WACHS / Government Staff" buyer-type and the Health Campus story all tie together nicely. That's a genuinely smart, locally-aware thread. I'd surface "GROH-approved" even harder given the hospital-driven demand — it's a real differentiator for this market.

**Opportunity:** the design names (Joey/Koala/BigRoo/Wombat) don't appear *anywhere* in the registration form's "Primary Dwelling" dropdown (which lists 2x1 ADU, 3x2, 4x2, 4x2+Theatre, 5x2, Dual Occupancy). A buyer falls in love with "BigRoo", scrolls to register, and there's no BigRoo to pick — it's "4x2 + Theatre Modular". Marry the marketing names to the form options, or you lose the emotional connection at the exact moment of commitment. (And the dropdown lists a "5x2 Modular Home" that has no card — phantom option.)

## The interactive subdivision plan (lot map)

Header "Select Your Preferred Lot(s)" with a good lead, an amber "all info is indicative / subject to final survey" disclaimer box, the plan reference, a STATUS legend (Available / 1 interested / 2 interested / 3+ interested / Reserved / Coming soon / Your selection), stage filter pills, a "145 lots · 38 reserved" line, view toggles (Plan view / Satellite / Schematic grid / Official drawing), and Purchase type / Land size / Price filters with an "Available only" checkbox (on by default).

I like a lot of this. The **"1 / 2 / 3+ interested" heat states are a clever scarcity mechanic** — showing a lot warming up with interest is exactly the nudge that gets a hesitant buyer to register before someone else does. The filters (purchase type, size band, price) are the right ones, and "Available only" defaulting on is sensible — though it does mean a buyer first sees "10 lots match — 135 dimmed", which momentarily reads as "only 10 lots in the whole estate". I'd soften that copy ("showing 10 available — toggle off 'Available only' to see all 145").

**The selection flow works and reserved lots are correctly blocked.** I clicked Lot 323 (the heritage lot you specifically wanted blocked) — it does NOT add to a registration; nothing happens, which is the right behaviour. I clicked Lot 310 and 311 (the $155k Stage 3 lots) — both open a clean detail modal and can be added. So the core gate (available selectable, reserved not) is behaving. One under-the-bonnet note: the reserved lots aren't actually `disabled` at the markup level (they just no-op on click), so a screen-reader user is told "button, Lot 323" with no "unavailable" state announced. Worth marking them properly disabled for accessibility.

**The map renders a phantom Lot 348 and is missing Lot 349.** The rendered plan shows "348" twice and there's no "349" anywhere — even though your lot data has a clean single 348 and simply has no 349 at all. So the front-end is duplicating 348 to fill a gap where 349 would be. A buyer who's been told "I want 349" by a friend, or an agent cross-referencing the deposited plan, will be confused. (If 349 genuinely doesn't exist in the final plan, fine — but then don't render a second 348 in its place.)

**The lot detail modal has a price contradiction.** Lot 310's modal shows "HOUSE + LAND: From $155,000" and "LAND ONLY: From $155,000" — the same number for both. A house can't be free. It's because the data has one price ($155k, land-only) and the modal prints it on both lines. Then, on the registration chip for the same lot, it says "No price set". So within one journey the same lot is "$155k for both" (modal) and "No price set" (chip). Pick one truthful presentation: land from $155k, H&L "from $485k+ (build quoted on your design)".

**Opportunity:** the satellite/official-drawing views are a nice touch for buyers who want to see the real land. If they aren't already, make sure each is captioned ("aerial — indicative lot overlay") so nobody mistakes a satellite image for the surveyed boundaries.

## Registration journey

This is the part that genuinely impressed me, and it's where you'll win against a tired "fill in your email" competitor. The flow is: click a lot → detail modal → "Add to my registration" → a per-lot configurator (Land vs H&L choice, a "Review & Set Your Price Expectation" step with price-sentiment buckets, lot detail incl. buildable footprint after R20 setbacks, and a "Primary Dwelling" picker) → then the contact form (name/email/phone/suburb/postcode), an "About You" block (buyer type, situation, living situation, timeframe, finance status, how-did-you-hear), a referrer section, notes, a required consent tick, and "Register My Interest".

Things I liked, as someone who has hand-keyed this data for years:
- **The qualifying questions are exactly what a sales team wants** — timeframe ("as soon as possible" → "just exploring"), finance status ("pre-approved" → "not yet started"), buyer type (incl. "Investor — Rental/SMSF" and "WACHS / Government Staff"). That's a lead I can prioritise the moment it lands. Most developer forms give me a name and a shrug.
- **The "buildable footprint after 1.5m setback" detail** on each lot is the single most-asked question I get ("what can I actually fit?"), answered before they ask. Excellent.
- **The price-expectation step** ("what would you expect to pay — this is not a commitment, it helps us gauge market") is smart, low-pressure market research that doubles as engagement.
- **The consent gate** is clear and states the consequence (registration only, indicative pricing, subject to survey) before you can submit — exactly right for an outreach-firing action.

Friction / concerns:
- **No "I am a real estate agent" path.** As a selling agent, I'd want to register a client and flag myself up front. The only agent hook is buried in the optional "Referrer Type" dropdown at the bottom. The "I am a..." list is buyer-only (First Home Buyer / Downsizer / Investor / WACHS). Add an agent option, or an "I'm registering on behalf of a client" toggle.
- **I could not complete a submission to a visible confirmation.** I filled every required field correctly (the consent tick went green, no field showed invalid), clicked "Register My Interest", and the form just... sat there — no "thank you", no error, no visible change. Your API endpoint itself is healthy (it answers in under a second and validates input server-side), so the back-end is fine; but from the buyer's seat, clicking submit and getting *no feedback at all* is a dead end. I genuinely couldn't tell whether my registration went through. Whatever the cause (a not-yet-set field in the price-expectation step, or a missing success/error message), **the one thing you cannot ship is a submit button that gives the user no response.** This needs a human to click through once end-to-end and confirm a real "thank you" screen appears — and that a failed submit shows a visible error, not silence. I'd treat this as the highest-priority item on the page.
- The "Current Suburb / Town" field is plain text. Minor, but an autocomplete would clean the data.

**Opportunity:** when a registration succeeds, the confirmation screen is a conversion moment most developers waste. Use it: "You're registered for Lot 310. Here's what happens next, here's Uwe's direct line, and here are two similar lots others are watching." Don't just say "thanks".

## Footer / terms

Privacy note ("not shared with any third party for marketing"), contacts for Uwe Jacobs and Dennis McMahon with direct phone + email, "Built by Factory2Key", a Privacy link, and "Real estate marketing only. No financial product is offered on this site." Sober and appropriate. Having two named humans with mobile numbers is good — buyers trust a name and a number.

**Opportunity:** the covenant is mentioned three times as "available on request / email Uwe". For a buyer doing diligence, a gated "request the covenant" button (that also captures their email) beats making them compose an email — and it's another soft registration.

## Would I register? — verdict

As a buyer: **yes, I'd want to** — the staged-release story, the price-by-size fairness, the buildable-footprint detail, and the modular designs are a compelling package, and a $155k flat serviced lot 8km from a CBD with a new hospital going in is a real proposition. But I'm not certain my registration would actually *go through*, because clicking submit gave me nothing back. That uncertainty is what would stop me, not the offer.

As a selling agent: I'd be comfortable sending clients here — it's credible and the lead form would feed me qualified buyers — **once** (a) the submit confirms it worked, (b) the lot counts reconcile, and (c) the design pricing is made comparable. Those three are what stand between "good page" and "page I'd stake my name on."

**The page earns the intent to register. It doesn't yet earn my confidence that the registration completed — and that's the gap to close first.**

## Standards Check

- **§1 Responsive (1440px)** — ✅ pass. Renders cleanly at 1440px, no horizontal scroll, layout is intentional (two-column blocks, comfortable line length), body text readable. (Did not stress-test mobile in this pass — laptop only per my brief.)
- **§5 Explanatory header** — ✅ pass. Every section opens with a what/why lead (About, Staging, Designs, the map, the registration form, the per-lot price-expectation step all have proper headers; empty state "Pick a Lot Above to Begin" keeps its explanation).
- **§6 Voice agent** — ❌ fail. No voice/assistant surface anywhere in the chrome (no FAB, no "ask a question" widget). The only help path is emailing/phoning Uwe or Dennis. For a page this nuanced (covenant, R20 setbacks, GROH eligibility, staged release), an in-context clarifier is exactly the kind of thing a buyer would use.
- **§7 Scaffold metadata** — ✅ pass. Browser tab title is "Seafields Estate — Register Your Interest | F2K". Not a scaffold default.
- **§9 Codicils (observable)** — 🟡 mixed.
  - Consequence clarity before an outreach-firing action: ✅ — the consent tick spells out "registration of interest only, no deposit, indicative pricing, subject to survey" before you can submit.
  - Zero dead ends: ❌ — the submit button gave no visible feedback (no confirmation, no error) after a fully-valid fill; that's a dead end at the most important click. Also the "No price set" vs "$155k" contradiction within one journey.
  - Address autocomplete: ❌ — "Current Suburb / Town" is a plain text field, not an autocomplete.
